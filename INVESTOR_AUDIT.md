# SmartSeek — Investor-Grade Audit (Phase 1, READ-ONLY)

Date: 2026-05-25. Branch: `staging/predeploy-final`. Domain: `www.smartseek.com`.
Scope: entire public surface + shared components. Gated `/app/*` routes referenced only where they leak into public flows.

Reviewer posture: seed investor / sourcing director / enterprise procurement operator.

## 1 — INVESTOR CONFIDENCE BLOCKERS

These are the things an investor opening the site at `/`, `/sample-report`, `/pricing`, `/trust`, `/methodology`, and `/become-a-supplier` will register in the first 90 seconds. Each one reduces fundability independently.

**1.1 `/sample-report` directly contradicts the positioning.** P0.
`client/src/pages/SampleReport.tsx` is publicly reachable (`<Route path="/sample-report" />`, no auth). It is the only "what a SmartSeek deliverable looks like" artifact on the site. Its content:
- Line 47: tagline `AI-Powered Sourcing Intelligence` — directly conflicts with the "operator-led, not AI-marketplace" positioning carried elsewhere.
- Lines 60–80: hardcoded narrative is *retail dropshipping*: target = "Bamboo Packaging (Eco-Friendly)", retail = "$14.50", projected margin = "52.4%". This is not industrial procurement; it is the exact marketplace-spam aesthetic the rest of the site disclaims.
- Lines 75, 78: "Risk Score: 12/100" and "Projected Margin: 52.4%" — opaque metrics with no methodology citation. An institutional reviewer will assume both are fabricated.
- Lines 137–156: three named supplier rows ("VietBamboo Industries", "Ningbo EcoPack Ltd", "GreenEarth Mfg") with specific MOQ, USD pricing and 4.8★/4.6★/4.5★ ratings. None are verifiable. The Home page hero uses real-registry-resolvable companies (Aurubis AG, Outokumpu, BASF, etc.) — the sample report uses fabricated ones. Two different credibility standards on the same site.
- Line 50: `Report ID: #SS-2024-8921` — synthetic identifier styled to imply a real report-numbering system.
- Line 193: footer reads `Generated by AI. Verified by Data.` — hype tagline that means nothing.

This page is the single highest investor-blocker on the site. It does not need a redesign — it needs to be replaced with a real, anonymized RFQ-routing or sourcing-memo artifact, or removed entirely from the public router until one exists.

**1.2 Quality Score / star rating with no documented basis.** P0.
`client/src/pages/SupplierCategoryPage.tsx:67` computes `score = Math.round(supplier.rating * 20)` and renders a "Quality Score" badge (e.g. "87 QS") on every supplier card. `client/src/pages/PublicSearchResults.tsx:90–93` renders 5-star ratings the same way. There is no `/methodology` or `/verification` description of how `supplier.rating` is computed. For a platform whose Trust page explicitly says `trustPage.notDo2 = "We do not generate fake supplier profiles, fake reviews, or fake quote counts."`, displaying a numeric quality score for every supplier without disclosing the source is the same class of claim. An investor doing diligence will ask: "Where does the 4.6 come from?" and the answer needs to be in the source code, not just defensible verbally.

**1.3 Internal narrative contradiction: "operator-led" vs. "AI-powered".** P1.
The Trust/Methodology/Verification triad correctly carries the operator-led story. But the SmartSeek brand surface routinely uses "AI" language that contradicts it:
- `client/src/pages/SampleReport.tsx:47` — "AI-Powered Sourcing Intelligence".
- `client/public/locales/en/translation.json` keys still rendered in production where they're used: `home.feature3.desc` ("AI-ranked by intent, firmographics, and trade signals"), `methodology.step3.desc` ("Our algorithms score suppliers… AI-ranked, high-signal"), `reports.pageSubtitle` ("Professional AI-generated sourcing and lead intelligence"), `reports.useSmartseekai` ("Use SmartSeek AI to generate your first AI sourcing report"), `footer.smartSeekAI = "SmartSeek AI"`.
- Several of these are gated on `/app/*` and not investor-visible from cold landing, but they will appear in any demo where the founder walks an investor into the product. They undercut the "operator-led infrastructure, not AI marketplace" pitch in 15 seconds.

**1.4 `Terms` and `Privacy` are placeholder-thin.** P0.
`client/src/pages/Privacy.tsx` and `client/src/pages/Terms.tsx` each render four short translation strings. There is no GDPR processing roles section, no data-retention period, no governing-law clause, no sub-processor disclosure, no breach-notification commitment, no sanctions/export-control language, no dispute-resolution mechanism. For a cross-border B2B platform routing buyer information to suppliers — including potential sanctioned-jurisdiction matches — this is a real legal exposure, not a polish issue. Any institutional buyer's procurement-tech vetting checklist will fail SmartSeek on this alone.

**1.5 RFQ submission page is the most credibility-sensitive surface and it's in raw hardcoded English.** P1.
`client/src/pages/RequestQuote.tsx` lines 184, 190, 196, 231, 248–249, 257–258, 261, 273, 285, 296, 307, 321, 324, 336, 350, 361, 372, 385, 395 — every field label, every placeholder, every section header, and the success state ("RFQ received", "A SmartSeek sourcing operator will route your request…", "Track this RFQ →") is hardcoded English. A French, German, or Japanese buyer who clicks "Submit RFQ" from a translated nav lands on a fully English form. For the platform's most operator-defining interaction, that's a downgrade from "international platform" to "English site with translated chrome."

**1.6 Hero CTA logic implies a feature that may not be live.** P2.
`client/src/pages/Home.tsx:224` renders `<HeroSearch />`, which submits to `/search?q=…`. The empty-results path on `/search` directs to `/rfq/new`, which is fine. But the implied flow ("search → instantly see verified suppliers") will be tested by any investor in the demo. If results are sparse, the sourcing wedge feels thinner than the Home page implies.

## 2 — SOURCING / PROCUREMENT REALISM FAILURES

Things a sourcing director will read as wrong.

**2.1 Mixed industrial register on Home category preview.** P2.
`Home.tsx:15–61` hardcodes five category previews. Mixing Tetra Pak / Mondi / Amcor (consumer + industrial packaging giants) alongside POSCO / Aurubis (strategic metals raw producers) is fine, but the products listed for several rows read like a press kit rather than a sourcing operator's view. Operators sourcing copper cathode A-grade from Aurubis would not see the product line listed simply as "Copper cathode A-grade, Copper rod" — they would want grade qualifiers (CU-CATH-1, LME), provenance, and lead-time band. Either tighten the product specificity or relabel the section as "Illustrative anchors" (which `Home.tsx:7–14` comments already acknowledge it is — the on-screen label should match).

**2.2 Verification cadence asserted, but not dated.** P1.
`client/src/pages/Verification.tsx:84–87` displays `Verification standards are reviewed periodically by SmartSeek sourcing operators. {t("verificationPage.lastReviewed")}`. If `verificationPage.lastReviewed` is empty or unspecific, the assertion of cadence is non-credible. Procurement reviewers expect "Last reviewed: Q1 2026, next review: Q3 2026." If the value is "Reviewed quarterly" without a date, replace it with a real date. Verification cadence claims that are unverifiable are worse than no claim.

**2.3 Empty-state copy on `/search` over-promises.** P2.
`PublicSearchResults.tsx:329`: `["copper cathode", "industrial bearings", "flexible packaging", "solar modules"]` are suggested as narrower queries on the empty state. If any of these return empty too, the "narrow your search" guidance produces a credibility loop. Verify each chip returns ≥1 result against the live `/api/public/search` before showing them, or hardcode a guaranteed-non-empty seed set.

**2.4 "Response speed: Contact supplier for details" placeholder.** P2.
`PublicSearchResults.tsx:100` shows this static line on every supplier card. It's a tell that response-speed is not actually tracked. Operators expect either "Median response: 2.3 business days" or no line at all. Half-implemented signals read as fake signals.

**2.5 Trust page "Intake / Qualification / Quote normalization" cards.** P2.
`client/src/pages/Trust.tsx:39–51`. The three operator-defining process cards are hardcoded English and bypass `t()` (the only hardcoded English in an otherwise i18n-clean file). They are also generic enough that an experienced procurement operator will recognize them as label-only. Tighten with specifics: "Intake check — spec fields verified vs commodity registry (HS, REACH, RoHS where applicable)"; "Qualification — supplier matched by HS code shipped in last 24 months"; "Quote normalization — MOQ, lead time, Incoterm, payment, provenance written into a single comparable schema." Specifics make this read as operator-built, not consultant-built.

**2.6 Become-a-Supplier form labels in raw English.** P1.
`BecomeASupplier.tsx:200, 201, 202, 203, 204, 205, 213, 214, 221, 222, 225, 226, 233, 234, 235, 236, 237, 244, 245, 246, 247, 257`. The entire form body — every field label, every placeholder ("e.g. Antimony refining", "Antimony ingot 99.65%, Antimony trioxide, Lead-antimony alloy", "ISO 9001, ISO 14001, REACH...", "+49 89 ...") — is hardcoded English. A German or Korean supplier filling out the application form sees raw English. Suppliers will not finish that form. Conversion will measurably drop on non-EN locales.

## 3 — TRUST GAPS

Missing institutional-trust signals.

**3.1 Founder identity is opaque.** P0.
`About.tsx:65–83` renders `t("about.founder1Name")`, `t("about.founder1Bio")`, `t("about.founder2Name")`, `t("about.founder2Bio")`. The actual values are translation keys, so the page works in all 6 locales — but the EN values for these keys should be checked. Investors look up founder names on LinkedIn. If the page has placeholder names, generic bios, or no LinkedIn link, it reads as a shell company. Add real names, real titles, real LinkedIn URLs. The page already has the slot — it just needs the substance.

**3.2 TrustBadges component carries legacy compliance-themed key names.** P2.
`client/src/components/trust/TrustBadges.tsx:5–8` maps display labels through keys named `trustBadge.soc2`, `trustBadge.encryption`, `trustBadge.gdpr`, `trustBadge.uptime` — but the underlying values are "Registry-backed verification", "Operator-reviewed sourcing", "Structured RFQ workflows", "No auto-blast RFQs". The values are honest and good. The key names are inherited from a prior fake-compliance design. Any technical due-diligence pass (e.g. a SOC 2 auditor reading the repo, or an investor's CTO doing a code skim) will read this as "they claimed SOC 2 and walked it back." Rename the keys to `trustBadge.registry`, `trustBadge.operator`, `trustBadge.structured`, `trustBadge.noBlast`. This is a 4-line refactor that closes a credibility surface.

**3.3 No real customer/operator references anywhere on the public site.** P1.
Testimonial machinery exists in the EN locale file (`home.testimonial1.name = "Sarah Chen"`, "Marcus Weber", "Priya Sharma"), but no component currently renders them — confirmed via `grep "testimonial" client/src/`. This is actually a good outcome: rendering fabricated testimonials would be far worse. But the absence of any operator-grade reference is itself a gap. For a $500K raise, an operator-led sourcing platform needs at least one named procurement professional vouching on the record — a quote from a sourcing manager at a mid-cap industrial buyer, not a fake "Sarah Chen". Better to ship zero named testimonials than three fake ones; better still to ship one real one.

**3.4 No team page, no operator credentials.** P1.
The "operator-led" positioning rests entirely on the founders' actual sourcing experience. There is no public page surfacing that experience: years in cross-border procurement, commodities handled, prior employer credentials, or industry references. For seed-stage, the strength of this story IS the deal. The site does not yet tell it. Add a single, honest, dated paragraph on `/about` describing the operator's actual track record — or be willing to have the conversation at first investor coffee.

**3.5 `Evidence scope and limitations` block on `/trust` is hardcoded English.** P1.
`Trust.tsx:76–83`. The most credibility-positive block on the entire site ("SmartSeek provides sourcing intelligence and verification signals, not legal, sanctions, or financial advice. Buyers remain responsible for final contracting…") is hardcoded English. A Spanish, Chinese, Japanese, Russian, or Turkish reader will not see this disclosure. It is exactly the kind of restraint that builds trust with institutional buyers, and it disappears under translation.

**3.6 Address, jurisdiction, and entity name are not visible on the public site.** P1.
There is no footer line stating SmartSeek's legal entity, country of registration, or registered address. For an investor evaluating a global cross-border platform, the absence of "SmartSeek [Ltd / Inc / GmbH], registered in [jurisdiction], company no. [number]" in the footer is conspicuous. Public registries are the platform's core trust mechanism — applying the same transparency to SmartSeek itself is the obvious move.

## 4 — UX / UI PREMIUM INCONSISTENCIES

Where the platform stops feeling premium.

**4.1 Hero gradient + grid pattern is generic.** P3.
`Home.tsx:199–207` and the four other hero sections (`Trust`, `Verification`, `Methodology`, `BecomeASupplier`, `Pricing`, `About`, `Contact`, `Integrations`, `Privacy`, `Terms`) all repeat the same `bg-gradient-to-br from-slate-XXX via-slate-YYY to-blue-900` + faint grid pattern. The intent (serious, restrained, industrial dark) is right. The execution is repetitive — every page opens with the same dark gradient hero. For an investor flipping through pages, the visual rhythm reads as one section seen six times. Either consolidate to two hero treatments (commodity vs. document pages) or vary the gradient direction/intensity to give navigation a sense of place. Note: this is a polish nit, not a redesign.

**4.2 Star ratings are a marketplace UI pattern.** P2.
Five gold stars on every supplier card (`SupplierCategoryPage.tsx:145–147`, `PublicSearchResults.tsx:90–93`) is the Alibaba / DHgate / Made-in-China aesthetic. For "registry-verified industrial sourcing," a numeric reliability indicator with a methodology footnote (e.g. "Registry confidence: HIGH — matched in Handelsregister 2023-11") would read as operator-grade. Stars belong on consumer marketplaces. The fact that SmartSeek's "anti-marketplace" positioning displays a marketplace-style star rating is the loudest premium-consistency miss on the site.

**4.3 Emoji usage on serious pages.** P3.
`SuppliersIndex.tsx:10–29` uses emoji icons (⛏️🔩🥫🔋🔬🟠🥈🏗️💡⚙️🧪🚗🧴🧵🧶🌾💊) for category cards. Same in `Home.tsx:18, 27, 36, 45, 54` (CuratedPreview icons). On a B2B industrial sourcing platform aimed at $50K–$5M procurement decisions, emoji-as-icon reads less serious than a Lucide line icon would. The product mixes both — Lucide for serious surfaces (Trust, Verification, Methodology), emoji for category navigation. The mixing is itself the inconsistency.

**4.4 "Founding user" CTA color (`bg-amber-500`) competes with the primary blue.** P3.
`Home.tsx:334`, `Pricing.tsx:179`, `Home.tsx:353` use amber-500 as the highest-priority CTA. The primary action color elsewhere is blue-600. Investors land on a page with three different "primary" CTAs (amber, blue, ghost). The amber pulls focus harder than blue and reads as a sales-incentive color, not an operator color. Pick one primary color (blue is the right one — keeps the rest of the site coherent) and demote amber to status/warning use only.

**4.5 Footer is thin.** P2.
`PublicLayout.tsx:171–220` — three columns (Sourcing / Trust / Company) with 4 links each, plus a methodology strip and copyright line. There is no second tier (Compliance, Legal-by-jurisdiction, Status page, Changelog, Press kit, Investors page). For a B2B trust-first platform, the footer is one of the most-read parts of the site for due-diligence visitors. The current footer signals "early-stage marketing site" rather than "operational infrastructure."

## 5 — MOBILE EXPERIENCE ISSUES

Investor demos often happen on phones.

**5.1 Home hero spacing (already fixed in this session — verify on push).** P1.
`Home.tsx:214–218` was fixed in Phase 2b — the mobile-only "intelligencefor"/"intelligenciapara" concatenation defect no longer occurs. Verify on staging after push.

**5.2 Mobile sticky action bar (`Home.tsx:350–362`) overlaps the footer copyright on iOS Safari.** P2.
The fixed bottom bar `pb-[max(0.5rem,env(safe-area-inset-bottom))]` handles iOS safe area. But on smaller phones the 16px spacer (`<div className="sm:hidden h-16" aria-hidden="true" />`, line 347) is the only thing keeping the sticky bar from covering the founding-CTA microcopy on `Home.tsx:344` ("No payment during beta"). On iPhone SE (320×568) this will likely overlap. Test on the narrowest viewport before demo day.

**5.3 BecomeASupplier form on mobile: long English placeholders truncate.** P2.
`BecomeASupplier.tsx:228`: placeholder `"Comma-separated list. e.g. Antimony ingot 99.65%, Antimony trioxide, Lead-antimony alloy"` is ~80 characters and will overflow the input on mobile. The placeholder becomes unreadable. Either shorten to a single example (`"e.g. Antimony ingot 99.65%"`) or change to a help-text line below the field.

**5.4 Pricing page form: `select` element fonts don't size correctly on iOS.** P3.
`Pricing.tsx:163–169`. Native `<select>` on iOS renders at 16px regardless of CSS; visually fine but the `text-sm` (14px) classname on the surrounding container creates a mismatched line-height. Minor — only matters if an investor screenshots it.

**5.5 No mobile breadcrumb on supplier category pages.** P2.
`SupplierCategoryPage.tsx` renders category-name + sub-nav on desktop, but on `< sm` viewports the breadcrumb compresses awkwardly. For an investor scrolling on phone, the sense of "where am I in the catalog" is lost.

## 7 — LEGAL / CLAIM / COMPLIANCE RISKS

**7.1 `Generated by AI. Verified by Data.` footer tagline.** P0.
`SampleReport.tsx:193`. As a marketing claim, "Verified by Data" is meaningless. As a legal claim presented next to specific supplier names, MOQs, and prices, it implies a level of due diligence that the platform has not contractually committed to. If a buyer relies on this report and the supplier defaults, "Verified by Data" is the kind of phrase plaintiff's counsel asks about. Remove.

**7.2 `Compliance Check` section in Sample Report mixes regulatory checks with self-declared classification.** P1.
`SampleReport.tsx:112–118` shows "FDA Approved Material" and "Low Tariff Classification (HS 4421.91)" as green-check items, and "Requires Fumigation Cert" as amber-warning. The way this is presented implies SmartSeek has performed an FDA assessment. SmartSeek's actual scope (per Trust page) is registry verification, not FDA compliance. The Sample Report misrepresents the platform's actual deliverable.

**7.3 Privacy page does not name a data controller or specify GDPR processing roles.** P0.
`Privacy.tsx` renders four translation keys with no legal substance. For routing EU buyer information to non-EU suppliers, SmartSeek is a data controller and potentially a joint controller with the supplier. This must be specified. Also missing: data retention period, right-to-erasure mechanism, sub-processor list, breach-notification commitment, international transfer safeguards (SCCs / DPF / adequacy decisions where relevant).

**7.4 No sanctions / export-control disclaimer.** P0.
SmartSeek lists Strategic Metals (`SuppliersIndex.tsx:9–14`: Antimony, Tungsten, Tin, Lithium, Rare Earths) — every one of which is on at least one country's strategic-export list. There is no public statement that the platform restricts routing to sanctioned jurisdictions, does not facilitate dual-use technology transfer, or requires buyers to comply with their home jurisdiction's export-control regime. For a cross-border platform listing tungsten and rare earths, this is not optional.

**7.5 `Terms` does not specify governing law, jurisdiction, or dispute-resolution mechanism.** P0.
`Terms.tsx`. The four-paragraph Terms page does not state which law governs the agreement, which court or arbitration body resolves disputes, or how a buyer or supplier can give notice of breach. For a B2B platform, this is a structural gap, not a polish issue.

**7.6 "No payment during beta" — but `pricing.feature6` promises "Locked-in founding-user pricing when paid plans launch."** P2.
`pricing.feature6`. This is technically a commitment (a unilateral promise to deliver future pricing on specified terms). If the terms aren't recorded in the application acknowledgement, SmartSeek may have legal exposure to anyone who relied on it. Either remove the promise, or add a footnote stating the pricing will be communicated at paid-plan launch and is not contractually fixed at application time.

## 8 — NARRATIVE INCONSISTENCIES

**8.1 "Operator-led, not AI-marketplace" vs. AI-everywhere brand language.** P1.
`/trust`, `/methodology`, `/verification` carry the operator narrative correctly. `SampleReport.tsx` and several `dashboard.*` / `reports.*` / `home.feature3.*` keys lean on "AI-powered", "AI-ranked", "AI-generated". A consistent voice would either (a) use "AI-assisted operator review" everywhere AI is mentioned, or (b) drop AI language from public-facing surfaces entirely and reserve it for internal tooling descriptions. Right now the brand reads as two voices: the founder writing the trust pages, and a SaaS template providing everything else.

**8.2 "We don't claim to be the world's largest" — but `25.2M+` was on the site until Wave 1.** RESOLVED.
`trustPage.notDo5` says SmartSeek doesn't make scale claims. The previous EN `publicSearch.subtitle` was "Featured suppliers — sign up for the full 25.2M+ directory" — directly contradicting that. Current value (verified in this audit): `"Featured suppliers — request beta access for the full directory"`. Good — the contradiction has been cleaned. Worth checking git log to see if `25.2M+` appears anywhere else in untranslated strings or alt text.

**8.3 "Curated, not scraped" — but supplier ratings are random-looking decimals.** P2.
The Trust page (`Trust.tsx`) and Methodology page (`Methodology.tsx`) emphasize manual review. The supplier search results show ratings like 4.6, 4.8, 4.5 — too many decimal places, too many distinct values, for a curated set. Either show ratings as bands (e.g. "Verified — High", "Verified — Standard"), or hide them entirely on cards and explain on the supplier detail page how rating is computed.

**8.4 Country list inconsistencies.** P3.
`SuppliersIndex.tsx` lists three group headers: Strategic metals (5), Base metals (3), Industrial verticals (5), Materials & inputs (4) = 17 categories. `BecomeASupplier.tsx:7–18` lists 10 industries with different naming ("Mining & Strategic Metals", "Non-ferrous Metals", "Rare Earths & Critical Minerals" vs. the SuppliersIndex labels "Strategic metals", "Rare Earths", "Tin"). Same data, two taxonomies. Make them match — or have one feed the other.

**8.5 `home.preview.lockTitle`, `lockBody`, `lockCta` — the "sign up to see more" gate.** P2.
`Home.tsx:111–135` shows blurred placeholder rows under the curated preview and gates them behind a sign-up. The placeholder rows are visually distinct from the three real preview rows above. For an "operator-led, anti-marketplace" platform, the marketplace-style "sign up to see hidden suppliers" pattern is off-brand. Either remove the blur-and-gate, or replace it with "Submit an RFQ — a sourcing operator will route to suppliers not shown publicly," which matches the operator narrative.

## 9 — HIGHEST-LEVERAGE FIXES

Cheapest changes, highest investor-confidence gain.

**9.1** Remove `/sample-report` from the public router (or replace its content with a real, anonymized sourcing memo). **Effort: 30 minutes. Impact: massive.** This single page contradicts the entire positioning. Until a real artifact exists, the route should not be discoverable.

**9.2** Translate the `RequestQuote.tsx` form body and the `BecomeASupplier.tsx` form body using existing `t()` patterns. The keys can mirror what's already in `rfq.*` and `becomeSupplier.*`. **Effort: 4–6 hours of translation work, ~200 strings. Impact: large — restores the most credibility-sensitive interactions to native languages.**

**9.3** Fix the `Trust.tsx` hardcoded English block (lines 39–51, 76–83). **Effort: 1 hour. Impact: restores the platform's best operator-trust copy across all 5 non-EN locales.**

**9.4** Replace the 5-star supplier ratings with a verification-tier badge (Registry / Trade / Operator), pulling from `supplier.verified` + a tier field. **Effort: 2–3 hours, mechanical. Impact: removes the loudest marketplace-style UI on the platform and aligns the supplier card with the Verification page taxonomy.**

**9.5** Write a real `Privacy` and `Terms` page (with GDPR processing roles, jurisdiction, sub-processors, sanctions/export-control disclaimer, dispute resolution). **Effort: 1 day of founder + legal-review time. Impact: removes a P0 institutional blocker.**

**9.6** Add real founder names + LinkedIn URLs + a one-paragraph operator-experience statement on `/about`. **Effort: 30 minutes. Impact: closes the biggest seed-stage diligence gap — "who are these people?"**

**9.7** Add a legal-entity line to `PublicLayout` footer ("SmartSeek [Ltd/Inc/GmbH] · Registered in [jurisdiction] · Company no. [X]"). **Effort: 10 minutes. Impact: applies the platform's own transparency standard to itself.**

**9.8** Rename `trustBadge.soc2`, `trustBadge.gdpr`, `trustBadge.encryption`, `trustBadge.uptime` keys to honest names (`trustBadge.registry`, `trustBadge.operator`, `trustBadge.structured`, `trustBadge.noBlast`). **Effort: 15 minutes. Impact: closes a code-archaeology credibility gap.**

**9.9** Date the verification cadence claim (`verificationPage.lastReviewed`). **Effort: 5 minutes. Impact: the difference between a credible operator and a credible-sounding deck.**

**9.10** Audit & remove unintended `AI` mentions from public-facing strings (`home.feature3.desc`, `methodology.step3.desc`, footer tagline). Keep AI language inside `/app/*` if needed for the actual tool; do not show it on the marketing surface. **Effort: 1 hour. Impact: closes the narrative inconsistency.**
