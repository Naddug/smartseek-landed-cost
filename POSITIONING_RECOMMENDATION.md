## ORTAQ — Category, Audience, and Messaging

*This document must be read after `CURRENT_PRODUCT_REALITY.md`.*

---

## THE FUNDAMENTAL CONSTRAINT

Before selecting a category or writing a headline, the most important business decision must be stated clearly:

**ORTAQ cannot claim AI capabilities it does not have.**

The "AI that reads your emails and understands your operations" vision is aspirational. It is the right long-term direction. But it is not the product today. A visitor who reads "ORTAQ reads your 47 emails and tells you what's happening" and then requests a demo will be shown a product that does not do this. That gap destroys trust at the highest-intent moment.

Every positioning recommendation below is therefore grounded in what a demo can honestly deliver — while creating the narrative architecture for the AI capabilities to land powerfully when they are built.

## THE CANDIDATE CATEGORIES

The user proposed these candidates. Each is evaluated honestly below.

---

### Candidate A: "Operational Memory"

**Concept:** ORTAQ is the system that remembers what happened, what was agreed, and where things stand — so teams don't have to reconstruct it from fragmented communications.

**Strengths:**
- "Memory" is a powerful, human word. Everyone understands it instantly.
- It implies continuity, context, and recall — the right product attributes.
- It is defensible today. A shared transaction workspace with full audit trail IS an operational memory system, even without AI ingestion.
- It does not overclaim AI. Memory can be structured (organized, recorded, accessible) without requiring machine learning.
- No competitor occupies this category name meaningfully.
- Scales with the product: when AI ingestion is built, "memory" becomes even more accurate.

**Weaknesses:**
- It could sound passive. "Memory" implies storage; the active value is surfacing and synthesis.
- A buyer might ask: "How is this different from a shared drive that also 'remembers'?"
- Requires the website to make "memory" active and specific — not just a word.

**Current product alignment:** HIGH. A deal workspace with audit trail, document history, and approval records is genuinely a memory system.

**Demo alignment:** HIGH. You can demonstrate operational memory with the current product vision.

---

### Candidate B: "Operational Intelligence"

**Concept:** ORTAQ provides intelligence about B2B operations — turning operational data into actionable understanding.

**Strengths:**
- Strong association with outcomes and insight.
- Positions ORTAQ in the same category as serious enterprise software.
- Sounds like a platform, not a tool.

**Weaknesses:**
- "Intelligence" heavily implies AI analysis. Without genuine AI, this is a difficult claim to support in a demo.
- "Business Intelligence" is already a crowded, specific software category (BI tools, dashboards, analytics). ORTAQ may be misclassified.
- Sounds like a consultant's report, not a product name.
- Asking "what intelligence does ORTAQ provide?" leads to expectations that cannot be met today.

**Current product alignment:** LOW. The current product design does not produce intelligence — it organizes information. Intelligence requires analysis.

**Demo alignment:** LOW. The gap between "intelligence" in the marketing and what the demo shows would be jarring.

**Verdict: Do not use this as the primary category now. Reserve for when AI ingestion is genuinely live.**

---

### Candidate C: "Operations Copilot" / "AI Operations Manager"

**Concept:** ORTAQ is an AI assistant that helps teams manage their B2B operations.

**Strengths:**
- "Copilot" is immediately understood — everyone has seen GitHub Copilot, Copilot in Office, etc.
- Implies active assistance, not passive storage.
- "AI Operations Manager" is a job title analogy — very easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- "Copilot" is severely saturated. Every B2B software is calling itself a copilot in 2026. It no longer differentiates.
- Both names require AI to be true. They are not honest descriptions of what the product does today.
- "Manager" implies autonomous decision-making the product does not provide.
- These names will feel empty if the demo shows a structured workspace without AI.

**Current product alignment:** VERY LOW. This is the most aspirational framing and has the largest gap with current reality.

**Demo alignment:** VERY LOW.

**Verdict: Do not use either name now. They are directionally correct but premature.**

---

### Candidate D: "B2B Operations System"

**Concept:** ORTAQ is a dedicated system for managing the operational complexity of B2B work — the execution layer that email, shared drives, and CRM do not cover.

**Strengths:**
- Highly accurate description of the product's actual scope.
- "B2B operations" is specific — excludes consumer, excludes internal project management.
- "System" implies infrastructure, not a lightweight tool.
- No overclaiming. Can be demonstrated fully with current product design.
- Scales well: when AI is added, it becomes "the AI-powered B2B operations system."

**Weaknesses:**
- Less emotionally compelling than "memory" or "intelligence."
- "System" can sound heavy or expensive.
- Somewhat generic — needs specific framing to differentiate from generic ERPs.

**Current product alignment:** HIGH. This is what the product is.

**Demo alignment:** HIGH.

## THE RECOMMENDATION

**Primary category: "Operational Memory"**
**Secondary descriptor: "for B2B teams"**

Full category statement: **"Operational memory for B2B teams"**

### Why "Operational Memory" wins:

**1. It is honest today and powerful tomorrow.**
A structured shared workspace with audit trail, document history, and approval records IS an operational memory system. This is true right now, with what can be built and demonstrated. When AI ingestion arrives, the word "memory" becomes even more accurate — memory is how AI-powered systems describe their context retention.

**2. It solves the right problem at the right level.**
The insight is "the information is there — the problem is that no one synthesizes it." Operational memory addresses this at the structural level: instead of distributed, fragmented information that requires reconstruction, ORTAQ creates a continuous, accessible operational record. This is achievable without AI. AI makes it dramatically better.

**3. It differentiates from every existing category.**
- Email is communication. It doesn't remember operationally.
- Shared drives are storage. They don't organize around operations.
- CRM is relationship tracking. It forgets after the contract.
- Project management tracks tasks. It doesn't track operational reality.
- ERP is transaction processing. It doesn't capture the human context.
- Generic AI is stateless. It has no memory between sessions.

"Operational memory" fills a real gap that every one of these tools leaves open.

**4. It creates the right ICP conversation.**
"Do you wish you could know what's happening in your operations without making five calls or reading 30 emails?" is the question that the positioning answers. Every B2B operations manager immediately says yes.

**5. It is a buildable category.**
Operational memory is a category ORTAQ can own. No major vendor is currently occupying it. The category can be defined on ORTAQ's terms.

## PRIMARY ICP

**Who this positioning is for:**

**Primary:** The operations director or head of trade at a Turkish manufacturing or trading company doing repeat business with international counterparties (buyers, suppliers, logistics partners).

Characteristics:
- Manages 5–30 active commercial relationships at any time
- Regularly asks "what's the current status on this?" because the answer requires checking 3–4 sources
- Loses time to reconstructing reality — finding which contract version was agreed, which approval is pending, who last communicated what
- Personally feels the cost of fragmentation (this is not a theoretical pain)
- Has authority to adopt a new system for their team
- Not a pure tech buyer — evaluates on business fit, not feature lists

**Secondary:** The CFO or CEO of the same company, who asks the operations director for status and gets an uncertain answer. This person is the economic buyer. They don't use the product daily but approve the purchase.

**Tertiary:** Mid-market international trade companies with professional operations teams (10–200 person companies), primarily Turkey-based for first market, with ambition to expand into broader B2B verticals as the product matures.

## PRIMARY BUYER

The operations manager / trade operations director, not the IT buyer or procurement manager.

This is a **business buyer**, not a tech buyer. The purchasing conversation is:
- "Does this solve a real problem I have today?"
- "Can my team actually use this?"
- "What happens to my data?"
- "Can I stop using it if it doesn't work?"

Not:
- "What's the API documentation?"
- "Does it integrate with our Salesforce?"
- "What's the SLA?"

This shapes the demo, the pricing page, the trust page, and the CTA strategy.

## MAIN PAIN

**The reconstruction problem.**

Every day, people managing B2B operations spend significant time reconstructing current reality. Before they can make a decision or answer a counterparty's question, they have to search emails, check WhatsApp, open a PDF, call a colleague, and piece together what's happening. This is not a discipline failure — it is a structural inevitability when operational information lives in fragmented, non-operational tools.

ORTAQ eliminates the reconstruction step. The operational picture is always current, always accessible, always complete.

Secondary pains:
- "Who agreed to this?" — Commitments made in email or WhatsApp are untracked and disputed
- "Which version is current?" — Document versioning chaos in shared drives and email attachments
- "Who's waiting on us?" — Responsibility tracking across counterparties with no clear owner
- "Can we prove what was agreed?" — Audit and dispute resolution requiring hours of email archaeology

## WHAT NOT TO SAY

The following claims must not appear on the website until the product genuinely delivers them:

- "ORTAQ reads your emails" (not implemented)
- "ORTAQ reads your WhatsApp messages" (explicitly denied on trust page — keep this denial, it's honest)
- "AI-powered" (no AI exists in the product)
- "Automatically extracts commitments" (not implemented)
- "Detects risks in communications" (not implemented)
- Any statistic about time saved that is not sourced from real user data

## THE AI DIRECTION — HOW TO HANDLE IT

The AI repositioning is the right long-term direction. The problem is timing. Here is the correct way to handle it on the website:

**State the direction, not the current capability.**

> "We are building AI intelligence into every operation — so ORTAQ can surface what's missing, flag what's at risk, and tell your team what to do next before they have to ask."

This is honest. It signals the product direction. It creates excitement for the vision. It does not claim AI capabilities that don't exist today.

When the AI capabilities are genuinely built and demonstrable in a live demo, the positioning shifts immediately:

> "ORTAQ reads your operational communications and tells you what's happening, what's missing, and what's at risk — without anyone having to piece it together."

That shift will be powerful precisely because the foundation (operational memory) was built first and established credibly.

## COMPETITIVE FRAMING

The comparison that wins: **vs. the current situation, not vs. other software.**

Most ORTAQ visitors are not evaluating it against a competing product. They are evaluating it against their current situation: email, WhatsApp, and shared drives. The competitive frame should be:

"What does your operation look like today?"
vs.
"What does it look like with ORTAQ?"

This is a more honest and more effective frame than "ORTAQ vs. CRM" or "ORTAQ vs. project management."

When competing categories come up (and they will in the FAQ and trust pages), the framing is:
- vs. email: "Email communicates. ORTAQ records and organizes."
- vs. shared drives: "Drives store files. ORTAQ builds operational context."
- vs. CRM: "CRM ends at the contract. ORTAQ manages what happens after."
- vs. project management: "Project tools track tasks. ORTAQ tracks operational reality."
- vs. generic AI: "Generic AI reads text. ORTAQ — eventually — understands operations." *(Only use this once AI is live.)*
